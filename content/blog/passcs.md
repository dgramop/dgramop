## the setup

George Mason's CS department had a problem. Teaching assistants were underpaid — $11/hr in 2021 — and students who needed one-on-one help couldn't get it. Office hours were overcrowded. Tutoring services were expensive and generic. The TAs who actually understood the material were burnt out and leaving.

I saw an opportunity in the gap.

## the offer

The pitch was simple: hire the TAs your university won't pay fairly, and put them in a room with students who'd rather not retake Algorithms. We paid our TAs $35/hr — more than triple the university rate — and charged students a flat fee per session with a guarantee: **pass or your money back.**

```
  ┌──────────┐    pays    ┌──────────┐
  │ student  │ ─────────▶ │ passCS   │
  └──────────┘            └────┬─────┘
        ▲                      │ wages (more)
        │ refund if fail       ▼
        │                 ┌──────────┐
        └──────────────── │  ta's    │
                          └──────────┘
```

The refund guarantee sounds reckless but it wasn't. Students who show up to tutoring sessions consistently almost always pass. The guarantee was mostly a marketing tool — it signaled confidence and lowered the barrier to sign up.

## growth

We grew to about 20 employees covering three subject areas: CS, math, and statistics. I handled ops — scheduling, payroll, marketing, the booking platform. The TAs handled the teaching. It worked because we stayed lean and let the product (good tutors, fair wages, accountability) do the selling.

## what i'd do differently

I'd have built the scheduling system earlier. We ran on spreadsheets and group chats for too long. By the time I wrote proper tooling, we'd already lost a few TAs to scheduling friction.

I'd also have expanded to other universities sooner. The model was portable — every CS department has underpaid TAs and overwhelmed students. But I was graduating and didn't want to run it remotely.

I sold passCS when I graduated. It continues to operate at GMU.
